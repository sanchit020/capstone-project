Tell me about supernova black hole

"A supernova doesn't *create* a black hole directly, although it's often involved in their creation story. A supernova is the explosive death of a massive star. If that star is sufficiently massive (generally more than about 20 times the mass of our sun), the core's immense gravity after the explosion can overcome the outward pressure, collapsing under its own weight to form a black hole. Therefore, a supernova is a *necessary event* in the creation of *some* black holes, not a black hole itself. The black hole is the *result* of the core collapse *following* the supernova. It's a dramatic end to a star's life, leading to one of the universe's most mysterious and powerful objects!"

"Prepare for a mind-bending journey, space explorer! Quasars, ah, quasars...they are among the most luminous objects in the known universe. Imagine a supermassive black hole, billions of times the mass of our Sun, at the heart of a young galaxy. As matter spirals into this behemoth, it gets incredibly heated and energized, releasing phenomenal amounts of energy across the electromagnetic spectrum – from radio waves to X-rays. This energy is what we see as a quasar, appearing as a point-like source of light, incredibly far from us. They are truly beacons from the early universe, offering invaluable insights into the formation and evolution of galaxies.
